# My College Path

I didn't have a five-year plan when I started thinking about college. What I had was a nagging curiosity about **how the world works under the hood** — why some risks turn into disasters, how small decisions compound, and whether you could actually *model* any of it with math instead of just hoping for the best.

That curiosity eventually took me to UC Berkeley's College of Computing, Data Science, and Society. This is the story of how I got there, what almost derailed me, and how I discovered that actuarial science and modern data work aren't as far apart as they first appear.

## The High School Spark

In high school I was the kid who actually enjoyed probability problems. While friends debated which AP history teacher was least painful, I was reading about expected value and wondering why insurance companies seemed to know the future.

I didn't know the word "actuary" yet. I just knew I liked turning messy real-world questions into numbers you could reason about.

When college application season arrived, I looked at traditional statistics programs, computer science, and a few "data science" experiments that were just getting started. Berkeley's new College of Computing, Data Science, and Society stood out. It wasn't just another CS degree with a stats minor bolted on — it treated data as a first-class way of understanding the world, with room for ethics, communication, and domain depth.

I applied. I got in. And then the real work began.

## Arriving at Berkeley (and Data 8)

The first semester hit hard in the best way.

The legendary **Data 8** course (Introduction to Data Science) was my gateway drug. Suddenly tables of real data, computational thinking, and statistical inference weren't separate subjects — they were tools you used in the same afternoon. I still remember the moment the idea of "simulation as a form of reasoning" clicked for me during one of the labs.

The college itself felt like the right home. We were encouraged to pair technical depth with questions like "should we even build this?" and "who is this helping?" That combination — rigorous methods + real stakes — is exactly what drew me to risk and insurance later.

Of course, there were the usual Berkeley realities: giant lectures, brutal p-sets, and the constant low-grade fear that everyone around you is a genius who started coding in kindergarten. I got through it the usual ways: late nights in Moffitt, study groups that turned into real friendships, and realizing that asking good questions in office hours is sometimes more valuable than pretending you already know the answer.

## The Pivot That Wasn't Really a Pivot

Somewhere in year two I started looking for internships that combined the modeling I loved with actual decisions that mattered financially.

I kept running into actuarial job descriptions. At first I dismissed them — "that's for people who take five exams and live in Excel, right?"

Then I looked closer.

Modern actuarial work, especially at a company like Next Insurance, is increasingly about:

- Building and validating predictive models for risk
- Using real telematics, claims, and customer behavior data
- Communicating uncertainty clearly to non-technical stakeholders
- Thinking in distributions and tail events instead of point estimates

This was *data science* wearing a different (very well paid) hat, with an extra layer of regulatory responsibility and long-term thinking that I found compelling.

I started studying for Exam P while still in school. The probability theory overlapped heavily with what I was already doing in stats and CS classes. The difference was the mindset: actuaries are trained to be *conservative* about the future in a way that pure tech sometimes isn't.

## What I Actually Learned

The biggest lessons weren't technical:

1. **You don't need to "pick one thing" forever.** Data skills transfer ridiculously well. The same mental models that help you clean a messy Kaggle dataset help you price a new insurance product or explain tail risk to a founder.

2. **Context beats raw intelligence.** The actuaries and data scientists I respect most aren't the ones with the fanciest models. They're the ones who ask "what could go wrong that the data hasn't shown us yet?"

3. **Writing is part of the job.** If you can't explain a model or a risk to a product manager or a regulator, the model is useless. Berkeley forced us to write constantly. I'm grateful.

4. **Community > curriculum.** The friends, TAs, and random people I met in the data science community at Berkeley have already shaped my career more than any single course.

## Where I Am Now (and What's Next)

I'm currently an Actuarial Analyst at Next Insurance. I spend my days building and reviewing models that help small businesses get the protection they need without paying for someone else's risk profile. The work sits right at the intersection I was looking for in college: real data, real money, real consequences, and constant learning.

I still take exams. I still write code. I still get surprised by what the data shows.

This blog is my attempt to write some of that down — the technical bits, the career reflections, the things I wish I'd known earlier, and the occasional rant about modeling assumptions that look great in a notebook and terrible in production.

If you're a student trying to figure out whether "data science" and "actuarial" are opposing forces or two sides of the same coin, or if you're early in your career and wondering what the actual day-to-day feels like: welcome. I hope something here helps.
